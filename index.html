Key Findings

Core Problem
89.7%
Nearly all paying users stop at trial and never come back
462 of 515 paying users transacted exactly once — a trial purchase. There is no post-trial moment that converts a user into a subscriber. The acquisition is working; the conversion layer doesn't exist yet.
Revenue Structure
48.4%
Trials and subscriptions are nearly equal — but subscriptions aren't sticking
~$9,300 from trials, ~$9,956 from subscriptions — almost a 50/50 split, which is actually a healthy starting point. The problem isn't the mix. It's that subscriptions churn at 76.2% before they compound, so every month the subscription base resets rather than grows.
Retention
76.2%
Churn rate makes $30k MRR unreachable at current pace
At 76.2% churn, we'd need to acquire 130+ new subscribers every single month just to hold flat at 30 active. Churn must come below 30% before we scale acquisition behind it.
Trend
-47.8%
Revenue fell 47.8% from October to February across complete months
Oct $5,095 → Feb $2,659. The Jan → Feb recovery of +21.4% shows the business responds to acquisition spend — but without subscriptions compounding, every month restarts from zero.
Attribution
88.4%
Almost no ad spend has attribution — ROAS is invisible
88.4% of all orders carry no UTM source tag. We cannot identify which campaign, channel, or creative drives paying users versus free-trial takers. We are optimizing ad spend for clicks, not conversions.
Opportunity
929
Free-trial pool is a near-zero-CAC re-engagement base
929 users took a free trial and never paid anything. A 3-email post-trial sequence (Day 1 / Day 4 / Day 10) at 10% conversion alone is 93 new paying users — from an audience we already own, at essentially no acquisition cost.

Fix the floor
The first-timer discount exists — make it reach people
The discount is live but there is no email trigger surfacing it after a trial ends — so almost no one sees it at the right moment. The immediate work: set up a 3-email post-trial drip (Day 1 / Day 4 / Day 10) that shows the discount to every trial user. This is the single missing piece between the offer existing and it actually converting. In parallel: add UTM parameters to every ad link so we know which campaigns drive paying users vs free-trial takers. Add a pause option in the cancellation flow so users who would cancel can hold instead.

Month 3–4
Build the engine
The preference form exists — find out if it's actually reducing churn
We have a preference form and provider allocation in place, which is a strong foundation. But 76.2% churn suggests the match isn't sticking. The first question to answer: are users who complete the preference form churning at a lower rate than those who don't? If yes — make completing it mandatory before any booking. If churn is similar either way, the allocation logic itself needs to improve, not the form. Beyond that: A/B test plan pricing ($49 vs $69 vs $89/month), introduce 3-month and 6-month plans at a 15% discount to lock in LTV upfront, and launch couples-specific ad creatives with a dedicated landing page — observed couples LTV is 4–5× the individual average.

Summary

We generated $19,301 across six months with a peak of $5,095 in October. The core problem is what happens after a user arrives: 89.7% of every paying user we have ever had made one transaction — a trial — and never came back. Subscription churn sits at 76.2%, meaning the moment we do convert someone, we lose them at nearly the same rate. The path to $30k MRR requires roughly 490 active subscribers. We have 15 today. This is not a marketing problem — it is a post-trial conversion and retention problem. Fix those two things first, instrument attribution second, then scale spend with confidence.
